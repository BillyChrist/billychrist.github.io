LEAPFROG

Lunar Entry and Approach Platform for Research on Ground

LEAPFROG is a full-scale autonomous lunar lander test platform developed at USC’s Space Engineering Research Center to support hardware-in-the-loop testing of guidance, navigation, and control systems.

My work focused on project leadership, system design and architecture, vehicle integration and testing, mechanical design, and development of embedded flight software for closed-loop control.

System Overview

Project Details and Involvement

  • Development of the vehicle required resolving several system-level design challenges spanning structures, propulsion, flight computing, and control system stability.

  • The landing leg assembly was redesigned to support repeated landings and survive a 1 meter drop test. Structural analysis and redesign of the suspension interface increased the survivable landing impulse while maintaining strict vehicle mass limits.

  • The original cold-gas reaction control system was replaced with an electrically driven attitude control system using eight electric ducted fans powered by rechargeable LiPo batteries. This eliminated reliance on consumables and enabled repeatable test operations without external gas infrastructure.

  • The flight computing architecture was upgraded from a single-board control solution to a distributed architecture using an STM32 microcontroller for real-time control tasks and a Raspberry Pi for high-level coordination, enabling RTOS-based control loops and ROS2 integration.

  • A control strategy was developed to resolve coupling between the turbine thrust vector control system and the attitude control system, preventing oscillatory behavior and allowing the two control systems to cooperate during stabilization.

  • Vehicle mass growth analysis showed that it exceeded the propulsion system lift margin. Component-level mass optimization and material selection were performed across the vehicle to maintain flight capability while preserving structural and functional requirements.

  • As project lead, I guided cross-disciplinary engineering teams and coordinated development across the project to advance the vehicle toward flight-ready testing.

Electronics

The vehicle avionics system was built around a distributed flight computing architecture combining an STM32 microcontroller for real-time control tasks and a Raspberry Pi for high-level command execution, telemetry, and system coordination.

The electronics stack integrates navigation sensors, propulsion control hardware, communications systems, and power distribution into a unified flight control network. Key components include multiple IMUs and a LIDAR sensor for state estimation, RFD900X radios for ground station communication, ESC-driven electric ducted fans for attitude control, and supporting propulsion and actuator hardware.

  • Developed the complete avionics architecture integrating flight computers, sensors, propulsion control hardware, communications systems, and power distribution.

  • Produced wiring schematics and interface documentation used to support rapid vehicle assembly, troubleshooting, and iterative testing.

  • Integrated key hardware including STM32 flight controller, Raspberry Pi flight computer, IMU array, LIDAR altimeter, RFD900X radios, ESC-driven EDF attitude control system, and supporting propulsion electronics.

Mechanical

The landing leg structure was redesigned to improve durability and enable repeated landing tests. The original mounting design concentrated loads at the swivel joint and relied on adhesive bonding, which created a structural weak point during high impulse loading.

A new landing leg mount was designed to mechanically secure the swivel joint while distributing landing loads into the surrounding side strut structure. The redesigned component was modeled in CAD, detailed through engineering drawings, and manufactured from Aluminum 6061-T4 using a 5-axis CNC milling process.

The figures on the right show the redesigned landing leg mount and a cross-section view highlighting the internal geometry used to redirect bending loads away from the joint interface.

  • Performed impulse force analysis for worst-case landing conditions (30 kg vehicle mass, 10 m/s velocity, approximately 6.8 kN impact force including margin).

  • Conducted finite element analysis comparing the original landing mount and the redesigned geometry, demonstrating improved stress distribution and reduced deformation at critical attachment points.

  • Iterated bracket geometry to survive worst-case loading while maintaining vehicle mass constraints.









The full landing leg assembly shown on the right includes the side-strut structure, compression and damping springs, and ball-joint mounting interfaces used to absorb landing loads.

  • Development, fabrication, and integration of the landing leg assembly incorporated side-strut linkages, spring-damper elements, and ball-joint mounting interfaces.

  • Performed component-level evaluation and material selection, implementing carbon-fiber structural members and nylon-T300 carbon fiber composite joints to maintain strength while minimizing vehicle mass.

Attitude Control System (ACS)

Vehicle attitude control was implemented using an eight-fan electric ducted fan (EDF) attitude control system regulated by a PID feedback controller. The controller processes orientation measurements from the IMU array and generates commanded power values for each fan group to stabilize roll, pitch, and yaw.

The controller architecture was modeled and validated using Simulink before implementation in the embedded flight software. The control loop computes proportional, integral, and derivative terms from the attitude error and distributes the resulting control outputs to the ESC-driven EDF actuators.

The figures on the right show the PID control equations used in the flight software, the Simulink block diagram used during controller development, and measured response data from hardware testing.

  • A three-axis test stand was developed to evaluate the attitude control system. The vehicle was mounted on a low-friction ball bearing fixture allowing near-frictionless rotation about the roll, pitch, and yaw axes.

  • Controlled perturbations of approximately 30° yaw, 10° pitch, and 5° roll were introduced while the controller actively stabilized the vehicle.

  • Measured response plots show the vehicle returning to equilibrium following the disturbance, along with the corresponding PID output commands distributed to the EDF actuators.

  • Controller tuning was performed through iterative hardware testing to achieve stable response while minimizing oscillation and overshoot.

Flight Software (FSW)

The flight software architecture separates real-time flight control from high level coordination using a dual-layer control stack. An STM32 microcontroller executes deterministic control loops under FreeRTOS, while a Raspberry Pi running ROS2 manages command handling, telemetry streaming, and mission coordination.

The STM32 embedded layer interfaces directly with flight hardware including IMUs, a LIDAR altimeter, thrust vectoring actuators, and ESC driven electric ducted fans. Independent FreeRTOS tasks manage attitude control, engine throttle regulation, thrust vector control, and telemetry handling, allowing deterministic execution and fault isolation for each subsystem.

The Raspberry Pi coordination layer acts as the bridge between the embedded flight controller and the external ground station. Commands received over the long-range RF radio link are decoded and forwarded to the STM32, while telemetry from the embedded controller is republished for real-time monitoring and logging.

The diagrams on the right illustrate the software architecture and the communication chain linking the ground station, coordination layer, and embedded control system.

  • Implemented a dual-layer control architecture combining FreeRTOS-based embedded control with ROS2 coordination and telemetry management.

  • Developed real-time firmware on the STM32 to execute ACS, TVC, engine throttle control, and telemetry tasks with deterministic scheduling.

  • Implemented a serial command protocol and heartbeat mechanism to maintain synchronization and detect communication faults between system layers.

  • Integrated ground station command handling and telemetry logging through ROS2 publisher and subscriber nodes.
