Spacecraft Systems Design

Kilometer-Class Space Telescope Mission Design

Space mission design study exploring the construction of a 1 km-class space telescope assembled in orbit. The project focused on the systems engineering required to transport and assemble the observatory, including launch architecture, orbital transfer vehicle design, subsystem trade studies, and mission-level cost and risk analysis.

My primary contributions focused on system-level architecture and mission feasibility analysis. I developed the spacecraft system block diagram and high-level subsystem layout, performed mission risk assessment and cost analysis, and conducted a detailed thermal system design study for the orbital transfer vehicle and telescope infrastructure. These analyses helped establish the overall spacecraft configuration and operational constraints used throughout the mission design study.

Mission Overview

This project explored the feasibility of constructing a kilometer-scale space telescope in orbit using modular components launched from Earth and assembled near the L2 point. The concept expands on the architecture of the James Webb Space Telescope by proposing a significantly larger segmented aperture that would be constructed in space from multiple launched structural and optical elements.

Our team was responsible for designing the orbital transfer vehicle (OTV) and logistics architecture required to transport telescope components from Earth orbit to the L2 assembly site. The study evaluated launch strategies, transportation infrastructure, and spacecraft systems needed to support a multi-launch construction campaign for a large-scale space observatory.

The mission analysis included launch vehicle selection, payload mass budgeting, trajectory planning, and spacecraft subsystem design. A dedicated OTV platform was developed to deliver structural elements, optical segments, and supporting hardware from launch vehicles operating in Earth orbit to the L2 construction orbit where the telescope would be assembled.

  • Developed mission architecture for in-space construction of a kilometer-scale telescope near the Sun–Earth L2 point.

  • Designed an orbital transfer vehicle (OTV) responsible for transporting telescope components from Earth orbit to the L2 assembly site.

  • Evaluated launch vehicle options and developed a multi-launch campaign using commercially available heavy-lift vehicles.

  • Performed payload mass budgeting and logistics analysis for transporting structural and optical components.

  • Conducted subsystem trade studies for propulsion, attitude control, communications, power, thermal, and command & data handling.

  • Analyzed mission cost, development timelines, and risk factors associated with large-scale orbital construction.

Risk Analysis

A structured mission risk assessment was developed to evaluate technical and operational risks across the spacecraft and overall mission architecture. Risks were identified at both the subsystem and mission level, then scored using likelihood and consequence criteria to determine which issues posed the greatest threat to mission success.

Pre-mitigation and post-mitigation risk matrices were used to compare baseline risk exposure against the expected impact of proposed mitigation strategies. This work helped highlight major system vulnerabilities, prioritize engineering attention, and show how redundancy, operational safeguards, and subsystem design choices could reduce mission risk during a large-scale orbital assembly campaign.

  • Identified mission and subsystem risks across mechanical, GN&C, C&DH, power, thermal, ACS, propulsion, telecom, payload, and full-system operations.

  • Developed mitigation strategies including redundancy, safe-mode behavior, sensor fusion, and standardized communication protocols.

  • Built pre- and post-mitigation risk matrices to compare baseline mission exposure against reduced-risk operational concepts.

Cost Analysis

A mission cost model was developed to estimate the overall financial scale of the observatory architecture, including both spacecraft development costs and the launch campaign required to deliver telescope components to orbit. The analysis combined subsystem-level estimates with lifecycle phase budgeting to capture the cost impact of design, development, integration, operations, and launch.

Launch campaign planning was based on the payload capacity and pricing structure of commercial heavy-lift vehicles, primarily Falcon Heavy. Payload mass limits and delivery cadence were used to estimate the number of launches required to transport mirror segments, structural components, and assembly infrastructure to Earth orbit before transfer to the L2 assembly site.

The resulting model provided a first-order estimate of the mission’s financial scale and helped identify the primary cost drivers within the architecture. Transportation logistics, spacecraft development, and assembly infrastructure emerged as the dominant contributors to total mission cost, highlighting the importance of launch efficiency and modular spacecraft design in enabling large-scale space observatory construction.

  • Estimated subsystem costs and life-cycle phase budgets.
  • Characterized major cost drivers including launch, assembly & integration, and early-phase design.
  • Summarized total mission costs with and without launch vehicle.

Thermal Design

The spacecraft thermal control system was designed to maintain critical subsystems within safe operating temperatures during both Earth-orbit operations and the final assembly phase near Sun–Earth L2. Thermal environments were evaluated across multiple mission phases, including low Earth orbit staging and deep-space operations, with expected temperature swings approaching ±120 °C. Eclipse durations and solar exposure conditions were analyzed to determine worst-case hot and cold scenarios for spacecraft components.

Heat transfer modeling was performed to estimate the total thermal load generated by avionics, propulsion hardware, and onboard systems. The spacecraft required approximately 9.1 kW of heat rejection capacity, which drove the sizing of the radiator system. Using radiative heat transfer analysis, the required radiator area was calculated to be approximately 2.4 m². The final design implemented three 1 m² radiator panels to provide sufficient margin and maintain stable thermal conditions throughout the mission.

Thermal control was implemented using a hybrid architecture combining passive and active components. Passive elements included multi-layer insulation (MLI), heat pipes, optical surface coatings, and radiator panels to regulate heat flow and reject excess thermal energy. Active control was provided through distributed Kapton surface heaters used to maintain stable temperatures for sensitive subsystems such as propellant tanks, batteries, and avionics modules.

Thermal coatings were selected based on their absorptivity and emissivity characteristics. Optical solar reflectors (OSR) were used on propellant tanks and radiator surfaces to minimize solar heat absorption while maximizing radiative cooling. White thermal paints (S13G-LO) were applied to avionics surfaces, while vapor-deposited aluminum coatings were used on structural base plates to control radiative properties and reduce thermal gradients across the spacecraft.

Several thermal architecture trade studies were conducted to evaluate passive, hybrid, and redundant thermal control approaches. While a purely passive system provided the lowest cost, a hybrid architecture with redundant thermal control elements was selected to improve system robustness and reduce mission risk. The additional redundancy increased system cost by only a small margin while significantly improving reliability during long-duration operations.

To validate the thermal design, a full spacecraft model was constructed using AutoCAD geometry and analyzed in ANSYS Thermal Desktop. Orbital simulations were performed to evaluate spacecraft temperature distributions at different points along the mission trajectory. The resulting simulations were visualized through synchronized animations showing spacecraft orbital position alongside the corresponding thermal response of the vehicle, demonstrating the effectiveness of the thermal control system throughout the orbital cycle.

  • Radiator panels: 3 × 1 m² for primary heat rejection

  • Kapton surface heaters: 42 distributed heaters for temperature regulation

  • Heat pipe network for thermal transport between subsystems and radiators

  • Multi-layer insulation (MLI): ~345 m² for thermal isolation

  • Thermal louvers for additional passive heat control

  • Sensors and thermal monitoring hardware integrated throughout the spacecraft
